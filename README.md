# How to Use the Essay Editor
## A Guide to AI-Assisted Writing — Where You Write, and the AI Helps

---

> **The core principle:** This tool does not write your essay. You do. The AI reads what you've written and offers observations, questions, and suggestions — the same role a good editor or trusted reader would play. Your voice, your ideas, your words.

## 2. Writing Your Essay

The editor uses **Markdown** — a simple formatting system. You don't need to know much to get started:

```
# This is your main title
## This is a subtitle or byline

A new paragraph is just a blank line between text blocks.

### This is a section heading

**Bold text** and *italic text* work like this.

---

A line of three dashes creates a section divider (used for Section review mode).
```

**As you write, focus on:**
- Your argument, your structure, your voice
- Getting your ideas down first — don't pause for perfection
- The AI will flag issues as you go; ignore the sidebar until a paragraph is done

**Word count** is displayed in the top toolbar and updates in real time.

## 3. Understanding Live Review

The Live Review panel is the heart of this tool. It watches what you write and, when you finish a paragraph or section, sends it to the AI for feedback — automatically.

### How it triggers

Review is triggered when:
- **Paragraph mode:** You press Enter twice (i.e., you leave a blank line after a paragraph)
- **Section mode:** You type `---` on its own line (a section divider)

You choose the mode in the header: **Review every → Paragraph / Section**

> **Paragraph mode** is best for granular, sentence-level feedback as you go.  
> **Section mode** is better for bigger-picture feedback once a whole idea is developed.

### Reading a review card

Each card is numbered and colour-coded. The same number appears in the **editor gutter** (the narrow strip to the left of the text area), so you always know which card belongs to which passage.

A card may contain any combination of:

| Label | What it means |
|---|---|
| **✦ Style & Flow** | Observations on voice, rhythm, word choice, or sentence structure |
| **◈ Impact** | Whether the point lands with force and clarity for the reader |
| **⚑ Accuracy & Nuance** | Claims that may need evidence, qualification, or further thought |
| **→ Suggestions** | 1–3 directional nudges — not rewrites, just things to consider |
| **⊕ References** | Real books, papers, or researchers that support or enrich the idea |

The AI only shows what's genuinely useful. An empty or minimal card means the passage is working well.

### Acting on a review card

- **Read it, then decide.** You are not obliged to act on any feedback.
- **Click the numbered badge** in the card header to jump the editor to that passage.
- **Click ↺ Re-run** to get fresh feedback after you've revised the paragraph.
- **Click ✕** to dismiss a card you've dealt with or don't find useful.
- **Collapse cards** by clicking the card header — keeps the panel tidy as cards accumulate.

## 4. Using the Chat Tab

The Chat tab is for direct, conversational dialogue with the AI about your writing. Use it when you want to **think something through**, not just receive feedback.

### How to use it effectively

**Select text first (optional but powerful)**

Highlight any passage in the editor before switching to Chat. The AI will see your selection and apply your question specifically to that passage rather than the whole essay.

You'll see a confirmation at the top of the chat input area: *Selected: "your highlighted text…"*

### What to ask

The Chat is most useful for:

- **Structural questions:** *"Does the argument in this section follow logically from the one before it?"*
- **Tone checks:** *"Is this paragraph too aggressive in tone for a LinkedIn audience?"*
- **Thinking out loud:** *"I'm not sure if this point is strong enough to carry a whole section — what do you think?"*
- **Specific rewrites of a passage you've already written:** *"This sentence feels clunky — how could I restructure it?"*
- **Research prompts:** *"What's the evidence base for the claim I'm making here?"*
- **Pressure-testing:** *"What's the strongest counterargument to what I'm saying in this paragraph?"*

### Applying suggestions

If the AI offers a rewrite of a specific passage, an **↳ Apply to essay** button will appear beneath the response. Clicking it will replace the selected passage with the AI's version in the editor.

> Use this sparingly. The best use of a rewrite suggestion is to read it, understand *why* it works differently, and then rewrite the passage yourself in your own words.

## 5. Configuring the Reviewer

Before you write (or at any point), visit the **Settings tab** to customise how the AI reads your work.

### Piece Overview

This is the most important setting. Tell the AI what your essay is trying to do:

- What is your central argument or thesis?
- Who is the intended reader?
- What tone are you going for?
- What is the emotional arc — where does the piece start and where does it want to leave the reader?

**Example:**
> *"This essay argues that measurement culture in the workplace dehumanised employees long before AI arrived. It builds toward the idea that redesigning for trust is a human choice we can still make. The reader is a senior business leader. The tone should feel like a wake-up call — honest but not preachy."*

The more specific you are here, the more contextual and useful the AI's feedback will be. Without this, each paragraph is reviewed in isolation.

### Reviewer Personality

Choose the type of feedback voice that's most useful for where you are in the writing process:

| Persona | Best used when… |
|---|---|
| **Sharp Editor** | You want honest, unsparing feedback. Good for late drafts. |
| **Writing Coach** | You want encouragement alongside critique. Good for early drafts or when confidence is low. |
| **Academic Reviewer** | You're making evidence-based claims and want rigour. |
| **LinkedIn Strategist** | You're optimising for reach and engagement on a professional platform. |
| **Devil's Advocate** | You want your argument stress-tested before you publish. |
| **Custom** | You have a specific reviewer in mind — describe them in your own words. |

Click **Apply Settings** after making changes.

## 6. Preview and Export

### Preview mode

Click **Preview** in the top-right header to see your essay rendered as formatted text — headings, bold, italics, and dividers all rendered properly. This is a good final check before publishing.

Click **Edit** to return to the writing view.

### Save as Markdown

Click **Save .md** to download your essay as a `.md` (Markdown) file. The filename is automatically generated from your essay title.

### Copy to clipboard

Click **Copy** to copy the full raw Markdown text to your clipboard, ready to paste into LinkedIn, Notion, Google Docs, or anywhere else.

## 7. Workflow Tips

**Start with a rough dump, not a polished opening.** Get your argument down in whatever order it comes. The AI can help you assess it — but only once it exists.

**Use Section mode for structural feedback, Paragraph mode for line-level feedback.** Switch between them at different stages of a draft.

**Set your Piece Overview before you start.** The AI's feedback is significantly more useful when it understands your intent, audience, and tone from the beginning.

**Don't let review cards interrupt your flow.** If a card appears mid-thought, ignore it. Finish the idea first, then come back to the feedback.

**Re-run reviews after revision.** Once you've reworked a paragraph, hit ↺ Re-run on its card to get fresh eyes on the revised version.

**Use Chat to think, not to outsource.** The best conversations in the Chat tab are ones where you're working through your own thinking — not asking the AI what to say next.

**Dismiss cards you've acted on.** Keep the review panel clean so you can see at a glance what still needs attention.

## 8. What the AI Will and Won't Do

Understanding these boundaries will help you get the most out of the tool.

### The AI will:
- Observe and comment on your writing
- Flag weaknesses in argument, style, rhythm, or evidence
- Ask questions that prompt you to think harder about your claims
- Offer directional suggestions (*"consider grounding this with an example"*)
- Point you to real sources and researchers relevant to your ideas
- Offer a rewritten version of a passage *when you ask for one*

### The AI won't:
- Write your essay for you
- Tell you what to argue
- Replace a paragraph with its own version unless you explicitly ask
- Invent sources or fabricate quotes
- Make your creative or editorial decisions for you

> The difference between a good writer using this tool and a lazy writer using this tool is this: the good writer reads the feedback, thinks about it, and then rewrites in their own words. The lazy writer clicks "Apply" and calls it done. Only one of them will have an essay that sounds like them.
